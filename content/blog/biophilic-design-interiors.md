## The Science of Nature-Inspired Interiors

Have you ever noticed how you feel calmer in a room with plants and natural light? That's not coincidence — it's biophilic design at work.

### What Is Biophilic Design?

Biophilic design is the practice of connecting building occupants more closely to nature. It's not just about adding plants to a room (though that helps). True biophilic design integrates natural elements into the very fabric of a space.

### The Six Biophilic Principles We Use

**1. Direct Nature Integration**
Living walls, indoor water features, and potted plants aren't decorative — they're essential. Research shows that rooms with living elements reduce stress by up to 15%.

**2. Natural Light Optimization**
We design spaces to capture daylight from multiple angles. South-facing windows (in Uganda's case, north-facing for balanced light) with light shelves that bounce daylight deeper into rooms.

**3. Material Authenticity**
Natural materials — wood, stone, bamboo, clay — signal "nature" to our brains on a subconscious level. We specify local Ugandan hardwoods, volcanic stone from the east, and handcrafted clay tiles.

**4. Spatial Variety**
Our brains evolved in savannahs with varied sight lines. Open-plan is great, but it needs alcoves, change in ceiling height, and visual connections between spaces.

**5. Natural Patterns and Shapes**
Straight lines are rare in nature. We incorporate curves, organic shapes, and fractal patterns that our brains find inherently calming.

**6. Refuge and Prospect**
We all need spaces where we can see without being seen (refuge) and spaces with broad views (prospect). A well-designed home offers both.

### Real Results

In our Otra Vida Hotel project, guest satisfaction scores increased by 22% after we redesigned the interiors using biophilic principles — proof that design that connects us to nature isn't just beautiful, it's good business.
